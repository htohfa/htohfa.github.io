Extracting Cosmology from the Lyman-α Forest with Wavelet Transforms Published

UC Riverside
Advisor: Prof. Simeon Bird
2023

The Lyman-α forest—absorption lines in quasar spectra—contains a wealth of cosmological information. But traditional two-point statistics (power spectra) miss the non-Gaussian information encoded in these cosmic "barcodes."

I applied wavelet scattering transforms to extract this hidden information, demonstrating an order-of-magnitude improvement in constraining cosmological parameters. This is crucial for upcoming surveys like DESI that will measure millions of these spectra.

Impact: Our method could help upcoming experiments achieve percent-level precision in measuring dark matter properties and the expansion history of the universe.

Testing Fundamental Physics with the Cosmic Microwave Background Published

Haverford College
Advisor: Prof. Daniel Grin
2020-2022
AIP SPS Outstanding Research Award

What if the fundamental constants of nature weren't actually constant? This seemingly wild idea has serious theoretical motivation and observational hints. I used the cosmic microwave background—our baby picture of the universe—to search for evidence that the fine-structure constant α has evolved over cosmic time.

Working with Planck 2018 data, I tested two theoretical models: the Bekenstein-Sandvik-Barrow-Magueijo model and the Runaway Dilaton from string theory. While we didn't find smoking-gun evidence for variation, we set some of the tightest constraints to date.

Why This Matters: If fundamental constants do vary, it would revolutionize our understanding of physics and potentially point to new physics beyond the Standard Model.

AI for Disease Surveillance: Mosquito Species Classification Industry Project

Microsoft Research
Mentor: Dr. Simon Frost
Summer 2022

Project Premonition aims to create a biological map of diseases using blood collected by mosquitoes. The challenge? Accurately identifying mosquito species from their wingbeat signatures using optical sensors that are highly sensitive to environmental noise.

I developed a noise-filtering system and designed a time-series LSTM network that significantly outperformed the existing GAN-based classifier. The key was distinguishing between actual species-specific wingbeat patterns and environmental noise changes.

Real-world Impact: This technology could help track disease vectors in real-time, potentially revolutionizing how we monitor and respond to vector-borne diseases like malaria and Zika.

Understanding AGN Feedback through X-ray Cavity Analysis Research Complete

MIT
Advisor: Prof. Michael McDonald
Summer 2021

Galaxy clusters should be cooling catastrophically, but they're not. Active galactic nuclei (AGN) are thought to provide the heating that balances this cooling through "bubbles" or cavities visible in X-ray observations.

I created simulations to test whether the observed correlation between cavity pressure and cluster luminosity is actually specific to AGN feedback, or just a generic property of the cluster gas. Turns out, it might be the latter—which suggests some "cavities" in telescope data might actually be numerical noise rather than real physical structures.

Key Finding: The pressure-luminosity correlation isn't unique to cavities—it's a general property of cluster gas, challenging our interpretation of these observations.

Exploring Exotic Physics with Holographic Methods

Bryn Mawr College
Advisor: Prof. Michael Schulz
2019-2021

Using the AdS/CFT correspondence—one of the most powerful tools in theoretical physics—I computed correlation functions for Lifshitz field theories. These exotic theories are relevant for understanding quantum phase transitions in condensed matter systems.

The beauty of this approach is that difficult quantum field theory calculations become manageable gravity problems in higher dimensions. I solved Klein-Gordon equations in curved spacetime to extract quantum field theory observables.

I also worked on plasma physics, using bispectral analysis to understand energy transfer in plasma systems—work that's now being used to study stability in galactic spiral arms.
